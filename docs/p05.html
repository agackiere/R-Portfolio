Bayesian Modeling Using the Framingham Heart Study Dataset

Data Source: Noeyislearning. (n.d.). Framingham heart study dataset. Kaggle. https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/noeyislearning/framingham-heart-study

Original Study: Dawber, T. R., Meadors, G. F., & Moore, F. E. (1951). Epidemiological approaches to heart disease: The Framingham Heart Study. American Journal of Public Health, 41(3), 279–286.

About: “The Framingham Heart Study Dataset is a comprehensive collection of patient information from an ongoing cardiovascular study in Framingham, Massachusetts. The dataset aims to predict the 10-year risk of future coronary heart disease (CHD) in patients based on various health attributes. It includes over 4,000 records and 15 attributes, making it a valuable resource for researchers and data scientists working on predictive modeling in healthcare,” (Noeyislearning, n.d.).

All Rhat values equal 1.00, indicating the MCMC chains converged properly. High n_eff values across all predictors confirm we have a sufficient effective sample size for reliable inference. After scaling continuous predictors, the results are more interpretable. Age is the strongest predictor (β = 0.6), meaning a one standard deviation increase in age meaningfully raises the log odds of developing coronary heart disease within ten years. Systolic blood pressure is the second strongest predictor (β = 0.4), consistent with its well-established role in cardiovascular risk. Being male adds approximately 0.5 on the log odds scale, reflecting known sex-based disparities in heart disease. Current smoker status has essentially no effect (β = 0.0), while cigarettes per day shows a modest positive association (β = 0.2). This suggests that smoking frequency matters more than smoking status alone for predicting CHD risk. Glucose (β = 0.2) and total cholesterol (β = 0.1) show smaller but positive associations. BMI and diabetes have wide posterior uncertainty, likely reflecting limited statistical power (diabetes in particular is rare in this dataset, making it difficult to estimate its effect precisely).

           "sysBP", "totChol", "BMI", "glucose", "diabetes"),
  prob = 0.8,
  point_est = "median"
)

age has the tallest, narrowest curve, high certainty, strong positive effect. sysBP and cigsPerDay are similarly tight. male is wider but clearly positive. currentSmoker is centered right on zero and spans both sides, we can say that the model is genuinely uncertain whether it has any effect. diabetes is that flat wide line at the bottom, so huge uncertainty, basically uninformative.

Model evaluation

pp_check(bayes_model, nreps = 50)

Simulated lines are tracking the actual outcome the simulated lines (y_rep) are tracking the actual outcome (y) closely, which means the model is capturing the data generating process reasonably well. The spike at 0 makes sense because most people in the dataset don’t develop CHD (class imbalance), and the model is picking that up.

Part 3: Informative Priors

We’re going to compare our weakly informative model (what we already have), an informative prior model based on literature, and see how much the data moved the posterior away from the prior.

A quick scan of the lit will tell us something of the sorts:

  • The original Framingham Heart Study papers (D’Agostino et al. (2008) “General Cardiovascular Risk Profile for Use in Primary Care” in Circulation) has actual coefficients for age, sex, blood pressure, smoking, cholesterol.

  • Wilson et al. (1998) “Prediction of Coronary Heart Disease Using Risk Factor Categories” also in Circulation with usable coefficients.

Strong positive effects: Age is a well established strong predictor, prior mean of 0.6, tight SD of 0.1. SysBP has strong evidence, prior mean of 0.4, SD of 0.1 Sex is also consistent across literature, prior mean of 0.5, SD of 0.15

Moderate effects: cigsPerDay, we’ll set prior mean of 0.2, SD of 0.1 glucose we’ll set the prior mean of 0.2, SD of 0.15. totChol has weaker evidence, so prior mean of 0.1, SD of 0.1

Weak/uncertain effects: BMI is surprisingly inconsistent in literature, prior mean of 0.1, SD of 0.2. currentSmoker is redundant with cigsPerDay, prior mean of 0.1, SD of 0.2. diabetes has a strong effect expected but rare, prior mean of 0.3, SD of 0.3

  predictor = c("age", "male", "sysBP", "cigsPerDay", "totChol",

  weak_prior = c(0.6, 0.5, 0.4, 0.2, 0.1, 0.0, 0.2, 0.1, 0.0),
  informative_prior = c(0.6, 0.5, 0.4, 0.2, 0.1, 0.0, 0.2, 0.2, 0.1)
) %>%
  pivot_longer(cols = c(weak_prior, informative_prior), 
               names_to = "model", values_to = "estimate")

ggplot(comparison, aes(x = estimate, y = predictor, color = model)) +
  geom_point(size = 3, position = position_dodge(width = 0.4)) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 0, linetype = "dashed", color = "plum4") +
  labs(
    title = "Posterior Estimates: Weak vs Informative Priors",
    subtitle = "Similar results suggest data dominates prior choice",
    x = "Posterior Mean (log odds)",
    y = NULL,
    color = "Model"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13)

The similarity between posterior estimates across prior specifications suggests that the dataset is sufficiently large to dominate prior influence, lending robustness to our conclusions. Though, importantly, this finding may not generalize to more diverse or smaller populations where prior specification becomes critical.
